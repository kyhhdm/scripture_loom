# Theological Brief — ECC-001: The Vanity of Life Under the Sun (Ecclesiastes 1:1–11)

**Passage's own emphasis (primary).** The Preacher, son of David and king in Jerusalem, opens with his thesis: "Vanity of vanities... all is vanity." He presses one question — "What does a man gain from all his labor... under the sun?" — and answers it by observation. Generations come and go while the earth abides; sun, wind, and rivers move ceaselessly yet only return to where they began. The passage stresses restless, profitless repetition: "there is nothing new under the sun," and even remembrance fails. Life "under the sun," taken by itself, yields no lasting gain.

**Key terms (from commentary).** *Vanity of vanities* — a Hebraism for the most utter vanity, the repetition intensifying (JFB); things vain not in themselves but when made the end instead of the means. *Under the sun* — this life, this present world, a phrase peculiar to Ecclesiastes. *Labor* — care and toil that wearies; profitable in its place, yet unable to supply the soul (MHC). *Gain/profit* — real, remaining benefit, which the creature cannot give.

**Doctrinal anchors.** No confessional proof-text cites this pericope; no doctrinal anchor is asserted. By WCF 1's method, the text is received as God's own inspired Word and read as sufficient and self-interpreting: Solomon speaks not in haste but "in God's name, and was divinely inspired to say it" (MHC).

**Reading moves (per dimension — especially D3/D7).** Wisdom-poetry. D7 = image → referent: read sun, wind, and rivers (vv.5–7) as figures for tireless, circular human striving that never arrives; note the turn at v.8 back to man's own labor and unsatisfied senses. D3 on the load-bearing terms *vanity*, *under the sun*, *gain/profit* — the passage's meaning turns on their repeated, technical use.
