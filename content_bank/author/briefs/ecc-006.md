# Theological Brief — ECC-006: The Common Fate of Man and Beast

**Passage's own emphasis (primary).** Solomon looks "under the sun" and sees wickedness lodged in the very place of judgment, where righteousness ought to sit (v16). He answers this twice: God will judge righteous and wicked, for there is a set time for every deed (v17); and God tests the sons of men so they see they are but beasts (v18). The proof is death — men and beasts share one breath, come from dust, return to dust, go to one place (v19-20). The skeptic shrugs, "who knows" whether man's spirit rises and the beast's descends (v21). The conclusion: nothing is better than to enjoy one's work, one's lot, since no one can show what comes after (v22). Without the fear of God, power and life alike are vanity.

**Key terms (from commentary).** "Place of judgment" — the courts that should be refuge but prove a prison to innocence (MHC). "God tests / manifest them" — fallen man's estate is ordered to prove men and show their mortal frailty, like beasts (JFB). "Sons of men" — "sons of Adam," fallen men (JFB). "Same breath / vitality" — as regards liability to death only, man is level with the beast if religion is excluded (JFB). "Who knows" — not doubt of the spirit's destination but how few consider the wide difference: man's spirit ascends, the beast's descends (JFB, cf. MHC). "His portion / lot" — thankful, cheerful enjoyment of God's gifts in this life, founded on the fear of God, not sensualism (JFB).

**Doctrinal anchors.** No confessional proof-text cites this pericope, so none is invented. By WCF 1.9, Scripture is its own infallible interpreter: the skeptic's "man is mere chance" (v19) and the unanswered "who knows" (v21) are settled within the canon itself — Ecc 12:7 plainly affirms the spirit returns to God who gave it, and v17 already names a coming judgment. The text is inspired and sufficient (WCF 1.4, 1.6); its plain sense is that death levels the body but not the soul.
