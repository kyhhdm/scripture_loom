# Theological Brief — MAT-034: The Leper's Prayer (MAT.8.1-4)

**Passage's own emphasis (primary).** A leper kneels before Jesus and confesses, "Lord, if You are willing, You can make me clean." He is certain of Christ's *power*; his one uncertainty is Christ's *will*. Jesus answers both: He reaches out and touches the man — which by the Levitical law would defile — says "I am willing... Be clean!", and immediately the leprosy is cleansed. Christ then directs the man to tell no one, to show himself to the priest, and to offer Moses' prescribed gift "as a testimony to them." The passage stresses Christ's willing, authoritative, word-alone power to cleanse, joined to His compassion and His care that the law be kept.

**Key terms (from commentary).** *Leper / leprosy* — a loathsome, spreading, humanly incurable disease, a familiar symbol of sin and its uncleanness (JFB). *Touched him* — Christ incurs the ceremonial defilement of contact (Lev 13; 5:3) yet transcends the statute, remaining "separate from sinners even when he lived among them" (MHC). *"If thou wilt... thou canst"* — striking faith sure of His power, seeking assurance of His will; Christ "sets His seal" to the confession of power and supplies the one thing doubted (JFB). *"I will; be thou clean"* — a word of kindness and of authority that works the cure immediately (MHC). *Testimony unto them* — a palpable witness that the Great Healer had come.

**Doctrinal anchors.** No confessional proof-text cites this pericope; per WCF 1.9 the text is read by Scripture's own clearer places rather than by an external doctrinal frame. Handled as inspired and sufficient, it teaches Christ's divine power and compassionate willingness to cleanse.

**Reading moves (per dimension — especially D3/D7).** Straightforward narrative; standard. D3 note only the freighted term *clean/cleansed* (ceremonial, not merely medical). D7 = trace the leper's "if You are willing / You can" and Christ's matching "I will; be clean" — the pivot of the account.

Off-agenda, set aside: the symbolic reading of leprosy as the soul's sin (MHC/JFB) is a legitimate typical application but secondary to the passage's own narrative emphasis; no doctrine beyond the lampposts is asserted.
