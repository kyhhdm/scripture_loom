# Theological Brief — MAT-047: The Twelve Apostles (MAT.10.1-4)

**Passage's own emphasis (primary).** Jesus calls his twelve disciples to him, gives them *authority* over unclean spirits and over every disease and sickness, and then the text names them, two by two. The stress falls on delegated authority that flows from Christ — and on the record of the names themselves. The list closes deliberately on "Judas Iscariot, who betrayed Jesus," so the passage holds together genuine commission and coming betrayal without softening either.

**Key terms (from commentary).** *Authority (exousian)* — Matthew Henry: "all rightful authority is derived from Jesus Christ," imparted power against the devil's kingdom (casting out) and for the world's healing (curing "all manner of sickness," none excepted). *Apostles* — "messengers," ambassadors "sent on an errand"; twelve, answering the twelve tribes of Israel. *Matthew the publican* — Henry: he brands himself the tax-collector "as if he would have all to know how deep a debtor he had been to his Lord." *Simon the Zealot* — JFB: "Kananite" is the Aramaic term for Zealot. *Betrayed him* — a "black brand" set on Judas's name, whom Christ took in knowingly.

**Doctrinal anchors.** No confessional proof-text cites this pericope, so none is asserted. By WCF ch.1 the naming and commissioning are received as inspired, sufficient, and self-interpreting: the parallel lists clarify, not correct, one another.
