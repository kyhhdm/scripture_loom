# Theological Brief — ECC-015: Wisdom and Folly Contrasted (ECC.10.1-11)

**Passage's own emphasis (primary).** Solomon presses the great advantage of wisdom over folly, and how easily folly undoes it. "A little folly outweighs wisdom and honor" as dead flies spoil the perfumer's oil (v1); the wise heart is ready "to the right," the fool's "to the left," and the fool exposes himself at every step (vv2-3). Turning to conduct under authority: when "the ruler's temper flares," do not abandon your post, for "calmness lays great offenses to rest" (v4). He names an "evil… under the sun" — an error proceeding from the ruler — that folly is set in high places while the worthy sit low (vv5-7). Then a string of proverbs: violent dealing recoils on the doer (digging a pit, breaching a wall, quarrying, splitting logs — vv8-9); "skill produces success" where brute strength endangers (v10); charm the snake before it bites (v11). The whole commends prudence, calm, and skill over force.

**Key terms (from commentary).** *A little folly / dead flies* — a small fault ruins a great reputation, "as hurtful to his good name as dead flies are to a sweet perfume" (MHC). *Right hand / left hand* — the wise man's heart is ready and dexterous, the fool's "always to seek" (MHC; JFB: the right hand is more expert). *Do not abandon your post* — "leave not thy place… do not in a resisting spirit withdraw from thy post of duty" (JFB). *Calmness / yielding pacifies* — mild submission lays great offenses to rest (JFB, via Pro 15:1). *The rich* — here "rich" carries the sense of wise, liberal, the antithesis of folly (JFB). *Skill produces success / wisdom to direct* — wisdom whets the tool rather than forcing matters "to one's own hurt" (MHC, JFB).

**Doctrinal anchors.** Method (WCF ch.1): receive this wisdom text as inspired and sufficient, letting clearer Scripture interpret its emblems. No confessional proof-text cites this pericope, so no confessional doctrine is anchored here; add none the lampposts do not support.

**Cross-references.** ECC.10.4 → PRO.25.15 (a soft tongue and long forbearing persuade a ruler); ECC.10.4 → ECC.8.3 (do not hastily leave the king's presence); ECC.10.10 → JAS.1.5 (ask God for the wisdom that directs); ECC.10.8 → PRO.26.27 (he who digs a pit falls into it); ECC.10.10 → COL.4.5 (walk in wisdom, redeeming the time); ECC.10.7 → PRO.19.10 (rule ill-fits a fool); ECC.10.1 → MAT.5.13-16 (a little folly can spoil a good witness, as salt losing savor).

**Reading moves.** D7 = image → referent: read each emblem (dead flies in oil, right/left hand, pit, wall, serpent, dull axe) to its point about folly's cost and force's recoil. Note the shift: wisdom vs folly in reputation and bearing (vv1-3) → conduct under a flawed ruler (vv4-7) → proverbs commending skill over force (vv8-11). D3 on the recurring terms "folly," "wisdom," "skill/success," and "under the sun."

Set aside as off-agenda: JFB's alternate rendering of v11 ("no gain to the enchanter") is a textual variant, not the plain sense followed here; and MHC's extended political counsel against rebellion is application beyond the text's own emphasis on prudence.
