# Theological Brief — MAT-011: The First Disciples (Matthew 4:18–22)

**Passage's own emphasis (primary).** Jesus, walking beside the Sea of Galilee, calls two pairs of fishermen brothers — Simon Peter and Andrew, then James and John — with the command "Come, follow Me," and the promise "I will make you fishers of men." The stress falls on the effectual authority of Christ's word and the immediacy and completeness of the response: "at once" and "immediately" they left nets, boat, and father to follow Him. The passage records a special, particular call to close and constant attendance, not merely a general summons to the crowd.

**Key terms (from commentary).** "Follow Me" — leave one's calling for close, constant attendance and imitation of Christ as Leader (Matthew Henry). "Fishers of men" — an allusion to their former trade; raised, as JFB notes, "from a lower to a higher fishing," to save men by gaining souls to Christ, not for wrath or gain. "Straightway / immediately" — Christ's word carries the power to make willing; He speaks and it is done.

**Doctrinal anchors.** Method (WCF 1): receive this narrative as the inspired, authentical Word of God, read in light of the parallel accounts (1.9). No confessional proof-text cites this pericope, so no doctrine is asserted beyond what the text plainly teaches: the sovereign efficacy of Christ's call and the obedience it works.

**Cross-references.**
- MRK.1.17-18 — parallel call; Mark's "Come ye after Me."
- JHN.12.26 — following Christ where He is.
- MAT.16.24 — deny self, take up the cross, follow.
- LUK.9.59 — another summons to "Follow Me."
- JHN.1.40-42 — earlier acquaintance with Christ (a distinct, prior meeting).
- MAT.10.2 — these fishermen later named among the apostles.

**Reading moves (per dimension).** Straightforward narrative; D3/D7 standard. Light D3 on "fishers of men" as the passage's one figure (trade → gospel labor); D7 traces the pattern call → immediate leaving → following across both pairs.

*Off-agenda, set aside:* commentary asides on ministerial ordination and qualifications for office are pastoral application beyond this passage's narrative emphasis.
