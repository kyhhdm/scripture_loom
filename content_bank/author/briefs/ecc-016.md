# Theological Brief — ECC-016: The Words of the Wise and the Fool (ECC.10.12-20)

**Passage's own emphasis (primary).** The passage sets the wise man's speech against the fool's and traces where each leads. A wise man's words are *gracious*; the fool's lips *consume him* — his talk begins in folly and ends in "evil madness," and he only multiplies words though no one can tell the future (vv.12-14). The same folly wearies him in fruitless toil (v.15). The theme widens to public life: a land is cursed under a childish, feasting ruler and blessed under noble princes who feast "at the proper time, for strength and not for drunkenness" (vv.16-17). Slothfulness lets the house — and the commonwealth — cave in (v.18); money "answers everything" for those who indulge (v.19); and even a private curse against the king may be carried by a bird (v.20).

**Key terms (from commentary).** *Gracious* — words that manifest and minister grace, doing good to hearers (MHC). *Evil madness* — the fool's speech grows violent and malicious, its "end" both its outcome and its aim (MHC). *A child* (of the king) — childish in understanding, not years; a nation may prosper under a young prince (JFB). *Eat in the morning* — the morning being the eastern hour for justice, here given over to feasting (JFB). *In due season* — refreshment taken only after duty (JFB). *Money answers all things* — as the instrument of commerce it meets every occasion of this present life, yet answers nothing to the soul (MHC).

**Doctrinal anchors.** No confessional proof-text cites this pericope; per WCF 1.6-1.7 its plain practical wisdom is drawn from the text itself and by good and necessary consequence, and per WCF 1.9 read alongside the clearer places below (Scripture interprets Scripture). No doctrine is added beyond what the text supports.

**Reading moves (per dimension — especially D3/D7).** Wisdom proverb-strings: D7 = read each saying as a compact image or contrast, then trace the widening scope from the fool's speech (vv.12-15) to public rule (vv.16-20). D3 on the sense-shift terms: "gracious," "a child," "in due season," "answers everything." Note the wise/fool antithesis becomes a blessing/woe on the land at v.16.

*Off-agenda, set aside:* JFB's figurative extension of v.20 to "the King of kings" who knows every thought (Ps.139) — a legitimate application, but beyond this verse's own point about earthly discretion, so not pressed here.
