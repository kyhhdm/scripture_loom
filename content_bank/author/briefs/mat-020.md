# Theological Brief — MAT-020: Oaths and Vows (Matthew 5:33–37)

**Passage's own emphasis (primary).** Jesus takes up the old command against false swearing and breaking oaths ("fulfill your vows to the Lord") and presses past it: "Do not swear at all." He forbids the evasive oaths of his day — by heaven, earth, Jerusalem, or one's own head — showing each is bound up with God, whose throne, footstool, city, and creative power these are. The disciple's plain word should carry its own truth: "Let your 'Yes' be 'Yes,' and your 'No,' 'No.'" Anything beyond that "comes from the evil one."

**Key terms (from commentary).** *Swear not at all* — not a ban on every oath (Matthew Henry and JFB both note lawful, solemn oaths remain), but on rash, frivolous swearing in common speech. *By heaven / earth / Jerusalem / head* — evasions that still profane God, since every oath "carries an appeal to God, whether named or not," and one's head is "beyond our control." *Yea, yea; nay, nay* — the disciple's known fidelity makes plain speech sufficient. *Cometh of evil* — from the corrupt, distrustful heart (JFB notably prefers this over "the evil one").

**Doctrinal anchors.** Reading Scripture by Scripture (WCF 1.9), Christ expounds the third commandment. WLC Q113 lists violating lawful vows and vain, irreverent swearing among its sins; WLC Q99 shows the law reaching the heart and speech, not words only. WCF 22.2 preserves the nuance: an oath "in matters of weight and moment" remains warranted under the New Testament — Christ forbids the abuse, not the lawful use.

**Reading moves (per dimension — especially D3/D7).** D7 = trace the "But I tell you" antithesis (old command → Christ's deepening), then the chain of oath-formulas each resolving back to God as referent; note the shift at v37 from prohibition to the positive rule. D3 = the sense of *swear* (solemn vs. frivolous) and *cometh of evil* — key terms the commentary qualifies; hold both readings lightly.
