# Theological brief — MAT-037: The Cost of Discipleship (MAT 8:18–22)

**Passage's own emphasis (primary).** As Jesus withdraws across the sea, two would-be followers meet his call, and the passage exposes what close discipleship actually costs. To the eager scribe who volunteers "I will follow You wherever You go," Jesus answers not with welcome but with warning: "the Son of Man has no place to lay His head." To the disciple who would first bury his father, he says, "Follow Me, and let the dead bury their own dead." The stress falls on Christ's poverty and his supreme claim: following him means counting the cost and giving him precedence over even the nearest earthly duty.

**Key terms (from commentary).** *Son of Man has no place to lay His head* — Christ's real, self-accepted poverty (MHC: "for our sakes he became poor"); he tests the scribe's sudden, worldly zeal. *Let the dead bury their dead* — a proverbial saying turning on two senses of "dead": let those dead to the kingdom of grace tend the naturally dead (JFB), while the called disciple preaches. *Follow Me* — for the second man, an effectual call; MHC notes we come "by the force of his call to us, not of our promises to him."

**Doctrinal anchors.** Method (WCF 1.9): let Scripture interpret Scripture — Luke's parallel opens the second man's word to preach the kingdom. No confessional proof-text cites this pericope; add no doctrine the lampposts do not support. Piety to Christ is preferred before piety to parents, yet the latter remains "a great and needful part of our religion" (MHC).

**Cross-references.**

- LUK 9:59–62 — the fuller parallel account of these callings.

**Reading moves (per dimension).** D7 = read the two encounters as a paired contrast (eager/rash vs. called/procrastinating), and hold the double sense of "dead" in v22 — the key is the higher-and-lower use of one word, not a slighting of filial love. D3 on *Son of Man*, *foxes/birds*, and *bury*.

Off-agenda, set aside: JFB's third (Wavering) disciple from Luke 9:61–62, which lies outside this pericope's text.
