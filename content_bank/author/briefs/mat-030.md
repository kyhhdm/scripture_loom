# Theological Brief — MAT-030: The Narrow Gate (MAT.7.13-14)

**Passage's own emphasis (primary).** Jesus issues a command with a reason: "Enter through the narrow gate." Two ways stand before every hearer, sharply opposed and mutually exclusive — no middle path. One gate is wide, its way broad, thronged with many, and it "leads to destruction." The other gate is small, its way narrow, and though it "leads to life," only a few find it. The stress falls on the summons to choose, on the difficulty and scarcity of the true way, and on the fatal ease of the false one.

**Key terms (from commentary).** *Enter through the narrow gate* — for Matthew Henry, conversion and regeneration, the "strait gate" of the new birth by which we pass from a state of sin to a state of grace; hard to find and hard to get through. *Narrow way* — the "afflicted way" of serious godliness, hedged by the divine law, requiring self-denial and endurance. *Wide gate / broad way* — easily entered and trodden, offering liberty for the appetites and abundant company. *Few find it* — few, JFB notes, because the whole course is as difficult as the first step, which crosses all natural inclination.

**Doctrinal anchors.** Treating the text as inspired and sufficient (WCF 1.4, 1.6), it plainly propounds what is "necessary to be known, believed, and observed, for salvation" (WCF 1.7): there are two destinies, and life is entered only by the narrow way. No confessional proof-text cites this pericope; add no doctrine the lampposts do not supply.

**Reading moves (per dimension — especially D3/D7).** D7 = image → referent: the gate/way pair is a spatial metaphor for two whole courses of life and their ends; hold the wide/narrow contrast throughout rather than allegorizing the details. D3 on the paired opposites — wide/broad/many/destruction versus small/narrow/few/life — noting the reversal that the easy way ends in ruin and the hard way in life.
