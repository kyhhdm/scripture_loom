# Theological Brief — ECC-002: Solomon's Quest for Wisdom (ECC.1.12-18)

**Passage's own emphasis (primary).** The Teacher, having reigned as king in Jerusalem, testifies from personal trial: he set his heart to seek and search out *by wisdom* all that is done under heaven, and found it a heavy God-given burden. The most promising human good — knowledge and learning — is itself weighed and found to be "vanity and a pursuit of the wind." His own unmatched wisdom could not straighten what is crooked nor supply what is lacking; increased knowledge only sharpened his grief. The stress falls on the insufficiency of even the greatest human wisdom to satisfy the soul or mend the world.

**Key terms (from commentary).** "Under the sun/heaven" — the whole sphere of human works and providence he investigated. "Sore travail" (v13) — a laborious, disciplining, humbling burden, knowledge won by the sweat of the brow (cf. Gen 3:19). "Crooked... cannot be made straight" (v15) — man's ways are hopelessly bent, beyond learning's power to reform; God alone straightens. "Vexation of spirit / pursuit of wind" — a preying upon, feeding on wind. "Madness and folly" (v17) — the works of human folly, studied to be avoided.

**Doctrinal anchors.** No confessional proof-text cites this pericope; per WCF 1.9 the text is read by clearer Scripture. Following its emphasis, the passage exposes that the light of human wisdom, though excellent in its place, cannot give the knowledge necessary for satisfaction or salvation — the very insufficiency WCF 1.1 assigns to nature apart from God's self-revelation.

**Reading moves (per dimension — especially D3/D7).** D3 (Vocabulary): pin the recurring figures — "under the sun," "vanity," "pursuit of the wind," "crooked/lacking" — and the commentary's sense that these mark insufficiency, not contempt for learning. D7 (Interpretation): reflective wisdom-monologue; trace the experiment (v12-13 setup) to its verdict (v14-15, v17-18), and hold the qualification that wisdom is "most excellent in its place" — the futility is of speculative human wisdom as a *chief good*, not of wisdom as such.
