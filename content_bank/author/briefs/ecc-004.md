# Theological Brief — ECC-004: The Vanity of Wisdom and Toil (ECC 2:12-26)

**Passage's own emphasis (primary).** The Preacher turns to weigh wisdom against folly. He grants a real advantage: "wisdom exceeds folly, just as light exceeds darkness" (v13) — the wise man's eyes are in his head, the fool walks in darkness (v14). Yet "one fate overcomes them both" (v14): both die, both are forgotten (v15-16). This levels his hope, so he "hated life" and hated his toil, because he must leave all he built skillfully to a successor who may be wise or foolish and never labored for it (v17-21) — "this too is futile, and a great evil." Such toil yields grief by day and no rest at night (v23). The turn comes at v24-26: there is nothing better than to eat, drink, and find enjoyment in one's work — and this good "is from the hand of God," given to the one "pleasing in His sight."

**Key terms (from commentary).** *Under the sun* — the horizon of this present life, where the whole trial is confined (MHC). *Futile / vanity* — no lasting profit or satisfaction, though wisdom is still better to have than not (MHC). *One fate / one event* — death, which befalls wise man and fool alike (JFB v14). *A great evil* — not evil in itself, this being the ordinary course, but evil as regards the chief good, that one toiled so fruitlessly (JFB v21). *From the hand of God* — both the good things and the heart to enjoy them are God's providential gift and grace (MHC v24-26).

**Doctrinal anchors.** By WCF 1.9 the inspired, sufficient Word interprets itself; the passage's own verdict — vanity resolving in God-given enjoyment — governs. WLC 136 (cited via ECC.2.22-23) reads the "distracting cares" and sorrowful, sleepless days of toil among the burdens the sixth commandment warns of, contributing only its reading of anxious, self-consuming labor. WLC 141 (cited via ECC.2.24) frames the cheerful, God-acknowledging use of one's calling — "a provident care... to get, keep, use, and dispose" what is convenient, with "moderation of our... affections concerning worldly goods."

**Cross-references.**
- ECC.2.26 -> PRO.13.22 — the sinner's wealth is stored up for the just.
- ECC.2.26 -> JOB.27.16-17 — the just wear what the wicked heaped up.
- ECC.2.26 -> PRO.28.8 — gains gathered are gathered for the gracious.
- ECC.2.26 -> 1CO.1.30-31 — true wisdom is Christ; glory only in the Lord.
- ECC.2.24 -> ECC.3.22 — nothing better than to enjoy one's work, one's portion.
- ECC.2.26 -> COL.3.16-17 — do all in thankfulness, as from God's hand.
- ECC.2.26 -> JHN.16.24 — ask, that your joy may be full.

**Reading moves (D3/D7).** Reflective wisdom: first-person weighing that resolves in a God-ward turn. D7 = trace the concession-then-limit — hold "wisdom exceeds folly" (v13) against "one fate overcomes them both" (v14), then follow the despair of v17-23 to its reversal at v24 ("Nothing is better... this is from the hand of God"). D3 = the evaluative refrains *under the sun*, *futile*, *one fate*, *from the hand of God* carry the argument; note *from the hand of God* marks the shift from vanity to gift.

Set aside as off-agenda: WLC 136's clauses on killing, war, and self-defense (only its "distracting cares / immoderate labor" reading fits); WLC 141's clauses on contracts, restitution, and lawsuits (only its ordered, cheerful use of one's calling and goods fits).
