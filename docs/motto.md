I would summarize the methodology in one sentence:

> **Treat the Bible as a language to become fluent in before treating it as a textbook to analyze.**

This changes almost everything about how we teach.

---

# The Bible Fluency Method

## Goal

The primary goal is **not** to finish a reading plan or master theology.

The goal is:

> **To become so familiar with the words, stories, people, and patterns of Scripture that reading the Bible becomes as natural as speaking a familiar language.**

Devotion and theology grow out of this foundation.

---

# Core Principles

### 1. The Bible is the textbook

Use the biblical text itself as the primary curriculum.

Commentaries, videos, and teachers are helpers—not the center.

---

### 2. Input before analysis

Just as children hear thousands of sentences before learning grammar, learners should first hear and read large portions of Scripture before detailed interpretation.

**Order:**

Hear → Remember → Recognize → Understand → Delight → Live

---

### 3. Build fluency before theology

Don't rush into abstract doctrines.

First learn:

* the stories
* the people
* the places
* the timeline
* the repeated words
* the flow of each biblical book

Then theology has a framework to fit into.

---

### 4. Observation before interpretation

Train learners to notice what is actually in the text.

Ask questions like:

* Who?
* Where?
* What happened?
* What was repeated?
* What surprised you?

Only later ask:

* Why?
* What does this teach?
* How should we apply it?

---

### 5. Read books continuously

Read through complete books over time rather than isolated passages.

This helps learners grasp the author's structure and argument instead of seeing disconnected stories.

---

### 6. Repetition creates fluency

Expect learners to encounter the same stories, themes, and vocabulary many times.

Repeated exposure strengthens long-term memory and deepens understanding.

---

### 7. Narration instead of constant lecturing

After reading, have learners retell the passage in their own words.

If they can narrate it accurately, they are beginning to own the text.

---

### 8. Build a biblical mental map

Each lesson contributes to a growing picture of Scripture through:

* timeline
* geography
* characters
* recurring vocabulary
* key verses
* connections within the book

---

### 9. Short, consistent routine

A predictable lesson might look like:

1. Opening prayer
2. Read the passage aloud
3. Narration
4. Review previous lesson
5. Timeline or map
6. Vocabulary
7. One key memory verse
8. One or two observation questions
9. Closing prayer

---

### 10. Separate Bible Class from Devotion

These serve different purposes.

**Bible Class**

* Become familiar with Scripture
* Build knowledge
* Read broadly
* Remember God's Word

**Devotion**

* Commune with God
* Meditate deeply
* Pray
* Apply Scripture personally

Both are essential and strengthen one another.

# The Learning Progression

```text
Stage 1
FAMILIARITY
"I know what's in the Bible."

↓

Stage 2
RECOGNITION
"I've seen this pattern before."

↓

Stage 3
UNDERSTANDING
"Now I understand why."

↓

Stage 4
MEDITATION
"This changes how I think and live."

↓

Stage 5
WISDOM
Scripture becomes part of one's life.
```

---

# The Teacher's Role

The teacher is **not primarily a lecturer**.

Instead, the teacher acts like a language coach:

* guides attention
* asks good questions
* encourages careful observation
* reinforces memory
* gradually introduces deeper understanding

The Bible remains the main voice in the classroom.

---

# A Guiding Motto

If I had to capture the whole philosophy in one sentence, it would be:

> **Read the Bible until its language becomes your language, its story becomes your story, and its way of seeing the world becomes your way of seeing the world.**

I think this approach aligns well with how Scripture itself was received in ancient Israel and the early church. Most believers first **heard** God's Word repeatedly in worship and family life, becoming deeply familiar with its language long before they had access to extensive study tools. The goal was not merely to know facts about the Bible, but to have "the word of Christ dwell in you richly" (Colossians 3:16). Bible fluency seeks to recover that pattern by laying a strong foundation of familiarity, upon which careful study, sound theology, and heartfelt devotion can naturally grow.
